Abstract

Crustal anisotropy is crucial to understanding the evolutionary history of Earth's lithosphere. Shear wave splitting of Moho P-to-S converted phases in receiver functions (RFs) have been often used to study crustal anisotropy. Harmonic variation of Moho Ps phases in delay times are used to infer splitting parameters of averaged anisotropy in the crust. However, crustal anisotropy may distribute at various levels within the crust due to complex deformational processes. Layered anisotropy requires careful investigation of the distribution of anisotropy before interpreting Moho Ps splitting. In this study, we show results from stations ARU in Russia, KIP in the Hawaiian Islands and LSA in Tibetan Plateau, where layered anisotropy is constrained well by intracrust Ps conversions at high frequencies using a harmonic-decomposition technique. Anisotropic velocity models are inferred by forward-modeling decomposed RF waveforms. We suggest that the harmonic variation of Moho Ps phases should always be investigated to check for anisotropic layering using RFs with frequency content above 1 Hz, rather than simply reporting averaged anisotropy of the whole crust.

Seismology

1 INTRODUCTION

Shear wave splitting has become one of the most powerful tools to study seismic anisotropy in the upper-mantle (Long & Silver 2008; Long 2013) or the core–mantle boundary region (Lynner and Long 2014). Splitting between SV and SH arrival times observed in teleseismic S waves has often been interpreted as a consequence of upper-mantle anisotropy since the contribution from the crust itself is typically small (Barruol & Mainprice 1993; Lev et al.2006). To estimate crustal anisotropy, shear wave splitting in P-to-S phases (referred as Ps splitting) converted from the Moho discontinuity has been widely used in receiver-function (RF) studies (McNamara & Owens 1993; Nagaya et al.2011; Chen et al.2013). Individual RFs include the radial component, which is sensitive to isotropic velocity change, and the transverse component, which is indicative of anisotropic layering or dipping interfaces. When the crust is anisotropic, P-to-S conversions from the Moho can produce P-to-SV and P-to-SH arrivals on the radial and transverse RF component, respectively, with an observable splitting time. Large spatial variations exist in the observations of delay times between Ps phases. For instance, McNamara & Owens (1993) observed splitting times of ∼0.2 s for crust beneath the Basin-and-Range Province of western North America, while delay times of ∼0.4 s are observed for eastern Tibetan crust (Kong et al.2016).

The Moho Ps-splitting measurement offers a way of studying averaged anisotropy above the Moho. The amount of anisotropy within the crust can be estimated from the Ps splitting times and the arrival times. Sun et al. (2012) suggest that both a velocity model with 6 per cent anisotropy in the lower crust and one with 5 per cent anisotropy in the whole crust can offer good fits to RF data observed in southeastern Tibet. The splitting measurements of the Moho Ps phases may lead to competing interpretations: (1) the Moho Ps splitting may be explained by average anisotropy of the whole crust; versus (2) the Moho Ps splitting may be indicative of a thin, anisotropic layer at the base of the crust; (3) the Moho Ps-splitting feature may also be caused by a highly anisotropic layer in the upper crust or middle crust, but an upper-crustal anisotropic layer may not be thick enough to cause significant delay times (Fig. 1). Distinguishing these interpretations is important for understanding crustal structures and their geophysical implications.

Ps phases converted from a boundary of an azimuthally anisotropic layer display a harmonic variation in amplitudes over backazimuths with a periodicity of π (‘four-lobed’ pattern) or 2π (‘two-lobed’ pattern) (Shiomi & Park 2008). The backazimuthal dependence of Ps amplitudes can be estimated by harmonic-decomposition analysis (Bianchi et al2010; Liu et al.2015; Olugboji & Park 2016; Park & Levin 2016a). In addition, the behaviour of Ps splitting in RFs is also dependent on the backazimuth of seismic events. Due to azimuthal anisotropy within the crust, the Moho Ps phases would exhibit harmonic moveout variations in arrival time (Liu & Niu 2012), which can produce four-lobed signals at Moho depth in harmonic stacks (Park & Levin 2016a). However, an anisotropic layer with subhorizontal symmetry axis at the base of the crust can produce Moho Ps conversions that resemble Moho Ps splitting. High-frequency harmonic RF stacks are able to reveal layered anisotropy at varied levels within the crust. Velocity models that are based on different interpretations can be evaluated by examining whether the synthetic RFs fit prominent signals in the harmonic stacks.

In this study, we analyse teleseismic data collected by long-standing stations, which provide excellent backazimuthal coverage for harmonic-decomposition analysis. Three different types of crust are investigated, including (1) continental crust with a normal Moho depth; (2) a doubly thickened continental crust; (3) an ocean-island crust. Frequency dependence of harmonic stacks is examined, which allows us to evaluate the inferred velocity models from forward modeling. High-frequency harmonic analysis offers important information on interpreting the observed Moho Ps splitting.

2 DATA AND METHOD

We use teleseismic data recorded by permanent stations ARU (Arti, Russia), LSA (Tibet, China) and an ocean-island station KIP (Hawaii, USA) from Global Seismic Network (Albuquerque Seismological Laboratory 1988; Liu et al.2008). ARU is located at the east margin of the East European Platform in the Uralian foredeep (Zonenshain et al.1984). LSA sits on the Lhasa terrane of Tibetan Plateau with a doubly thickened crust as a result of India–Eurasia collision (Yin & Harrison 2000). KIP is located on Oahu, Hawaii, a hotspot island in the central Pacific. We collect a total of 2300 seismic records with epicentral distance 30° < Δ < 95° and magnitude larger than 5.9 (Fig. 2). All records are high passed at f = 0.03 Hz to eliminate long-period noises.

2.1 RFs processing

We estimate RFs in the frequency domain using multitaper spectrum correlation, which is resistant to spectral leakage (Park & Levin 2000). We obtain the first three Slepian eigentapers by using a time–bandwidth product P = 2.5. We calculate eigenspectra by applying the eigentapers to the first 80 s of seismic data series. We compute RFs by deconvolving the horizontal components from the vertical component via a least-squares correlation between the eigenspectra. Performing the inverse fast Fourier transform on the frequency-domain RFs obtains the time-domain RFs. First-order moveout corrections are performed using the IASP91 reference model (Helffrich 2006; Park & Levin 2016b).

2.2 Harmonic-decomposition analysis

The harmonic-decomposition technique has been applied to RFs to study isotropic and anisotropic layering (Bianchi et al.2010; Park & Levin 2016a). Harmonic expansion reveals two-lobed and four-lobed backazimuth dependence, providing constraints on the orientation of anisotropy or dipping structures. The RF harmonics are computed by performing a linear regression of radial and transverse RF amplitudes. We assume that the backazimuth variation of RF amplitudes at each time step can be modeled as a scaled sum of cos(kϕ) and sin(kϕ), in which k denotes the harmonic order and ϕ denotes the backazimuth. k = 0 represents the isotropic velocity contrast. k = 1 and 2 correspond to RF backazimuth variation with a periodicity of 2π (two-lobe) and π (four-lobe), respectively. The backazimuth variation of radial and transverse RF amplitudes at the same time step displays a phase shift of π/2k (Shiomi & Park 2008; Schulte-Pelkum & Mahan 2014; Licciardi & Piana Agostinetti 2016). To enhance the features of anisotropy or dipping interfaces, we obtain the ‘dip/anisotropy’ panel by stacking the radial and transverse RFs with a phase shift of +π/2k in backazimuth. Stacking the radial and transverse RFs with a negative phase shift of −π/2k produces the ‘unmodeled’ panel, in which effects of anisotropy or dipping structures are suppressed.

Frequency dependence of the harmonic RF stacks are examined with a maximum cut-off frequency of 4 Hz. Uncertainties are estimated by bootstrap resampling the data set for 100 realizations. In addition, we rotate the seismic records to L-Q-T system to enhance converted phases from shallow depths (Reading et al.2003).

2.3 Forward modeling

In this study, we use forward modeling to examine various models by fitting prominent two-lobed and/or four-lobed signals observed in harmonic stacks. Synthetic seismograms are computed using the ANIREC reflectivity code for 1-D hexagonal anisotropic structures (Chen 1993; Levin & Park 1997). Anisotropic models are constructed by augmenting isotropic velocity models with anisotropic parameters including strike (θ) and tilt (δ) of symmetry axes, and percentage of anisotropy. A positive percentage means that the unique symmetry axis is a fast axis, and vice versa. The strike and tilt of symmetry axes are inferred from two-lobed and four-lobed signal patterns. The magnitudes of anisotropy are constrained by fitting the amplitudes of Ps phases. We choose a Vp/Vs ratio of 1.70 for all crustal models. We also evaluate the goodness-of-fit based on the cross-correlation coefficients between the synthetic and real RF time-series. Azimuthal anisotropy within the crust can cause a harmonic moveout of Moho Ps phases in arrival time, which has been used to estimate splitting parameters of crustal anisotropy (Liu & Niu 2012; Sun et al.2015; Wang et al. 2016). The expressions of the cosine moveout are primarily four-lobed signals at Moho Ps arrival time on decomposed harmonic stacks (Fig. 3). However, interpreting these four-lobed signals as strong Ps splitting is not the only possibility. An anisotropic layer at the base of the crust can create Ps conversions at the Moho, which can also produce four-lobed signals on harmonic stacks (Fig. 4). Distinguishing different interpretations should be feasible with an inversion technique, but in this paper we distinguish the hypotheses by careful model evaluation.

3 RESULTS

We compute high-frequency RFs that reveal intracrust structures beneath each station. RFs are grouped into 10° backazimuth bins spaced by 5° overlapping to investigate backazimuthal variation. We present frequency-dependent harmonic RF stacks, which allow observations for thin, anisotropic crustal layers. In addition, we show synthetic harmonic RF stacks for different anisotropic models. Although there may be other solutions of anisotropic parameters for each model due to the non-uniqueness of forward modeling, the locations of anisotropic layers are constrained by prominent Ps signals on harmonic RF stacks. By comparing with observed RFs, the best-fit model offers robust constraints on the interpretations of anisotropic RF signals.

3.1 ARU

RFs are computed with a cut-off frequency of 2 Hz and plotted against backazimuth to examine backazimuth dependence (Fig. 5). Radial RFs show clear Moho Ps conversions at ∼5 s with a cosine moveout in arrival time. Low Ps amplitudes of Moho phases occur at ϕ = 130°–210°, which can be caused by the Moho Ps conversions due to anisotropy, similar to the feature we observe in synthetic results for a crust model with a basal anisotropic layer (Fig. 4).

Positive phases at ∼4 s define a layer at the base of the crust. Transverse RF signals with a polarity switch between 4 and 5 s are indicative of azimuthal anisotropy in the basal layer. Negative pulses at ∼2.5 s occur later than the reverberated phase (∼1.6 s) from the interface at ∼0.4 s (Fig. 7a), suggesting a low-velocity zone (LVZ) in the middle crust.

Fig. 6 shows the frequency-dependent harmonic stacks. The harmonic moveout of Moho arrivals would cause mainly four-lobed signals, therefore the prominent two-lobed and four-lobed energies observed at ∼4.8 s indicate either averaged anisotropy in the whole crust or layered anisotropy in the lower crust. At lower frequencies (<1 Hz), correlation coefficients of major RF signals in the harmonic stacks are larger than 0.6, indicating a good fit by model A21 with 5 per cent anisotropy throughout the whole crust (Fig. 7b). However, this model cannot fit the two-lobed signals at ∼4 s in RF stacks with higher cut-off frequencies (>2 Hz), suggesting layered anisotropy rather than averaged anisotropy in the crust beneath ARU. Model A11 that contains an anisotropic layer at the base of the crust provides a good fit to data (Fig. 7a). Note that high-amplitude reverberated phases at ∼1–1.5 s in the synthetic RFs are not observed in real data because the interface at ∼0.4 s may not be a perfectly flat reflector in real earth. No anisotropy is observed for the LVZ, suggesting that the velocity drop may be caused by a compositional or thermal feature. If it is a thermal feature, the LVZ could be related to partial melt or the subsolidus rheology effects of grain boundary sliding.

3.2 KIP

The oceanic Moho is observed at ∼2 s on the radial RFs (Fig. 8), consistent with Olugboji & Park (2016). The backazimuth dependence of RFs shows no strong evidence of Moho harmonic moveout or Ps splitting (Fig. 8). This is not surprising because the oceanic crust beneath KIP is too thin to accumulate substantial birefringence. In the higher frequency band of harmonic stacks (>2 Hz), a thin layer at the base of the oceanic crust is identified at ∼1.7–2.5 s, similar to the structure observed for ARU. In addition, two-lobed and four-lobed signals at ∼1.7–2.5 s are suggestive of azimuthal anisotropy in this layer. Prominent signals are well fit by model K06 that comprises a thin, anisotropic basal layer (Fig. 9).

The KIP RFs display significant amplitude at t = 0 delay time on the transverse component. This behaviour could arise from a direct P wave that is refracted by a dipping interface at depth. One or more dipping interfaces are geologically plausible beneath KIP, associated with, for example, layering within the volcanic edifice or thickness variations in the underplated layer described by Leahy et al. (2010). However, seismic evidence argues against a dipping layer as the cause of the two-lobed Ps signals near 2 s delay in Figs 8 and 9. Shiomi & Park (2008) discovered that harmonic stacks for dipping slab interface beneath the Kii peninsula of Japan displayed significant amplitude in the ‘unmodeled’ harmonic stacks, caused by two-lobed dip-related Ps moveout variations that accompany the two-lobed Ps amplitude variations, see also Park & Levin (2016a). Shiomi & Park (2008) demonstrated that this moveout could be modeled to constrain the orientation of the slab dip. For KIP, the RFs in Fig. 9 do not display significant Ps amplitude on the ‘unmodeled’ harmonic stack near 2 s time delay, which argues that the ∼2 s delay Ps feature on the ‘anisotropy/dip’ harmonic stack is not caused by a dipping interface. The transverse-component RF signals at t = 0 at KIP may be caused by topographic variations in the rock surface surrounding KIP, associated with its siting on Oahu island.

3.3 LSA

Fig. 10 shows the backazimuthal variation of RFs computed with fc = 1 Hz. Moho Ps phases with weak harmonic moveout are identified at ∼10 s, corresponding to a depth of ∼80 km (Kind et al.2002). No strong evidence of transverse RF signals are observed for the basal layer at ∼7–10 s delay time, indicating that the harmonic moveout of Moho phases may be caused by anisotropy in the upper-to-middle crust. Velocity reductions in the mid-crust are indicated by the negative pulses at ∼4 and ∼6 s. The negative pulse at ∼6 s should not indicate reverberations from shallower interfaces, because a reverberated phase from the interface at ∼2 s would be expected to be positive. The harmonic RF stacks show strong two-lobed and four-lobed energies before ∼4 s and strong four-lobed signals at ∼5–7 s, suggesting layered anisotropy in the upper and middle crust. Major signals on the harmonic stacks of fc = 1 Hz can be fit by model LS11, which includes three anisotropic layers at 0–18, 18–24 and 42–50 km (Fig. 11). These anisotropic layers have different symmetry-axis orientations. The addition of Ps splitting associated with these disparate anisotropic geometries could explain the weak, but non-zero, harmonic moveout of Moho phases.

4 DISCUSSION AND SUMMARY

Ps splitting in RFs has been widely used to study crustal anisotropy, but individual RFs with observable Ps splitting between the radial and transverse components are often rare (e.g. McNamara & Owens 1993). The backazimuthal variation of Moho Ps arrivals in RFs has been used to constrain splitting parameters of crustal anisotropy (Liu & Niu 2012; Sun et al.2012; Wang et al.2016). Shear wave splitting of Moho Ps phases will integrate through layered anisotropy within the crust. An accurate anisotropic velocity model, whether it contains thin layers with strong anisotropy or a near-uniform weak anisotropy, has an important impact on geophysical interpretations. Useful models of crustal formation and dynamics rely on careful investigation of the distribution of anisotropy within the crust.

Interpretations of Moho Ps splitting depend on whether anisotropy is nearly uniform in the crust or distributed at different levels. Silver & Savage (1994) showed that their shear wave splitting data observed at the San Andreas Fault can be explained well by a two-layered anisotropic model, in which the first anisotropic layer may correspond to an anisotropic crust. Shear wave birefringence integrates variations in anisotropic strength with depth, but ordinarily the simple interpretation of uniform crustal deformation will be taken. Layered anisotropy may develop in more complex crustal tectonic processes. In a compressional tectonic zone such as Tibetan Plateau, viscous crustal flow may occur in middle-to-lower crust, causing deformation and metamorphism that could produce complex layered anisotropy (Ozacar & Zandt 2004; Sherrington et al.2004; Liu et al.2015; Shen et al.2015). Porter et al. (2011) observed strong lower crustal anisotropy in southern California whose RFs could be modeled with a thin, anisotropic basal layer, indicating underplated anisotropic schists during subduction. Endrun et al. (2011) suggest that extensional deformation in the deep Aegean crust may also cause distributed viscous flow leading to complex layered anisotropy. In addition, Audet (2015) observed strongly layered anisotropy in the upper and middle crust around the San Andreas Fault, which may indicate anisotropic mica or serpentine in sheared layers. Thus, locating anisotropic layers with intracrust Ps conversions is crucial to understanding the dynamic evolution of the crust.

In this study, we are able to locate anisotropic layers at various depths using a frequency-dependent harmonic-decomposition technique. For instance, the harmonic behaviour of ARU Moho arrivals observed at low frequencies can be explained by 5 per cent uniform anisotropy in the whole crust. However, higher resolution RF signals reveal an interface at ∼3.5 s (Fig. 7) which can be modeled well by an anisotropic (8 per cent) basal layer and little or no anisotropy above it. We observe a similar situation for KIP, an ocean-island seismic station. Although the oceanic crust beneath KIP is much thinner than a normal continental crust, a thin basal layer can be imaged with high cut-off frequencies (>3 Hz). We confirm the interpretation of Olugboji & Park (2016) that a subcrustal anisotropic layer lies beneath station KIP and likely, via the lateral extension of the underplated layer reported by Leahy et al. (2010), beneath much of the Hawaii plume swell.

In both cases using higher resolution RFs, we find that major anisotropic signals occur in the basal layer, causing us to reject the least-information hypothesis of a uniformly anisotropic crust. For a normal continental crust like ARU, RFs with frequencies lower than 1 Hz may provide estimates of averaged crustal anisotropy, but RFs with high frequencies of ∼1–2 Hz could reveal important evidence of layered anisotropy that could motivate a more sophisticated tectonic model. For a typical oceanic crust like KIP, RFs with frequency content above 3 Hz might be needed to image thin layers with thicknesses of ∼5–6 km. Our analysis suggests that it is crucial to investigate crustal anisotropy using RFs with higher frequency content.

In contrast, no significant anisotropy is observed for the basal layer beneath station LSA in southern Tibet. Although harmonic variation of Moho arrivals in delay time is weaker than the previous two cases, strongly layered anisotropy occurs in the upper–middle crust at ∼0–30 and ∼40–50 km, suggesting that limited Ps splitting may not necessarily imply weak crustal anisotropy. For a thick crust, intracrust Ps conversions should be considered to avoid underestimating crustal anisotropy when analysing the shear wave splitting of Moho Ps phases.
